Externalization and Internalization

Learning is optimized when AI is utilized to make implicit human thinking visible (externalization) and convert machine models into internal mental structures (internalization).

Crucial Implementation Caveats (PDP Critiques)

The UX-Learning Tension

Over-designing friction checkpoints can yield acute user fatigue. If students must complete excessive reflection forms, they will simply use an external AI agent to generate the reflection itself—triggering a secondary offloading loop. Friction must be targeted strictly at critical conceptual pivots.

The Scaffold Dependency Loop

If co-regulatory prompts remain permanently directive, students experience an illusion of capability but fall victim to SRL collapse (learned helplessness) when the scaffolding tools are withdrawn. Designers must establish an algorithmic "decay rate" on scaffolding alerts.

Designed for Education in the Era of Agentic and Generative AI

I. IMPLICIT STRUCTURE: PHILOSOPHY & VALUES

1.1 Statement of Pedagogical Philosophy

The signature pedagogy for the era of Hybrid Intelligence (HI) is anchored in Agentic Symbiosis—a theoretical worldview stating that learning is not merely the frictionless acquisition of information or the automated optimization of performance, but a deliberate, relational process of selective cognitive delegation, active epistemic monitoring, and progressive internalization (Yan & Gašević, 2026).

In an educational landscape saturated with agentic and generative artificial intelligence, traditional indicators of learning (such as successful task completion or flawless essay outputs) are decoupled from actual cognitive change. If a student can delegate the entirety of their cognitive work to an AI system, "successful performance can no longer be assumed to indicate learning" (Yan & Gašević, 2026).

Therefore, our pedagogy shifts the focus of educational design from productivity to epistemic growth. It treats the human-AI interaction not as an automation pipeline, but as a dialogic relationship where human agency is central, and AI is utilized as a dynamic scaffolding partner (Molenaar, 2022) and an epistemic mirror to externalize and internalize complex representations (Cukurova, 2024).

1.2 Core Pedagogical Values & Dispositions

  • Epistemic Agency (Active Ownership): Learners must cultivate the intentional disposition to drive, structure, and own their cognitive journeys. This value demands that learners view themselves as the ultimate authors of their understanding, resisting the urge to passively offload critical thinking, reasoning, and planning to automated systems (Viberg et al., 2025). It prioritizes agency-first task designs where human goal-setting precedes machine execution.
  • Epistemic Vigilance (Critical Skepticism): In a world of highly fluent, plausible, yet fallible generative models, learners must maintain a stance of systematic doubt and rigorous cross-examination (Fan et al., 2024). Rather than treating AI outputs as authoritative answers, they must be approached as raw materials, cognitive provocations, or divergent ideas requiring thorough verification and contextual grounding.
  • Cognitive Courage (Valuing Friction): True learning requires schema reconstruction, which is inherently challenging and carries high cognitive load. Learners must develop the disposition to embrace desirable difficulties and resist "metacognitive laziness"—the tendency to rely on AI to bypass the productive struggle necessary to construct long-term memory structures (Fan et al., 2024).

II. DEEP STRUCTURE: EPISTEMIC & PEDAGOGICAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES

2.1 Theoretical Grounding Matrix Summary

The paper establishes four central Epistemic Principles (EP) derived from research in self-regulation and hybrid intelligence, matching them directly to actionable Pedagogical Design Principles (PDP):

PDP1: Introduce Desirable Epistemic Friction. Set up mandatory checkpoint boundaries for manual evaluation, synthesis audits, and delta statement logs to curb laziness.

PDP2: Scaffold Shared Regulatory Agency. Transition planning and evaluation responsibility dynamically via the HHAIR model.

PDP3: Mandate Double-Loop Explanations. Students must explicitly trace mental-model changes against AI proposals.

PDP4: Enforce Agentic Delegation Protocols. Declare formal task boundaries via Delegation Contracts prior to collaboration.

2.2 Critical Trade-offs & Sociodemographic Constraints

In under-resourced or low-digital-literacy settings, complex hybrid intelligence frameworks risk widening the equity gap. If learners lack foundational domain knowledge, they cannot perform the "epistemic monitoring" required by PDP3. They are highly susceptible to accepting AI-generated hallucinations as absolute truths. Therefore, foundational, non-AI conceptual training must always precede agentic human-AI collaboration.

III. SURFACE STRUCTURE: THE DESIGN PATTERN LANGUAGE

3.1 Derived Design Narratives & "The Accidental Scribe"

Source Scenario: In a graduate-level ed-tech seminar, students conducted a literature review on hybrid systems. Armed with agentic search tools, they quickly submitted polished syntheses but failed basic conceptual debate during oral exams. They had successfully offloaded the thinking process.

The Intervention: The class implemented a mandatory "Delegation Protocol." Before launching AI queries, students drafted a manual hand-drawn concept map and completed a formal delegation contract. They logged three critiques for every AI output generated, which restored oral mastery levels.

3.2 Component Design Patterns

These components operate in synergy. Pattern 3 (The Delegation Protocol) prepares boundaries and ownership, which feeds directly into Pattern 1 (The Epistemic Checkpoint), which outputs data and tracking values to calibrate Pattern 2 (Dynamic Co-regulatory Scaffold).
